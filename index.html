Part One Self-Reflection

A look at how college has gone so far, how I have struggled in the past, and what actually motivates me to do the work.

College Experience So Far

  • This is my first year at PSU and I am living in the University Pointe apartments, not official PSU housing, but on campus and more private. Having my own place this close to school gives me a lot of freedom, but it also means that I am fully responsible for managing my time and money. Paying rent is an intense bill each month that I'm not used to. It also means making sure I am always on top of my work, with full responsibility for my routines. There is no parent in the next room reminding me about homework.
  • Academically, the biggest change has been the pace and the amount of reading and writing. In high school I could usually get away with starting things late and still doing well. Deadlines were less strict, late policies weren't always enforced and it allowed me to build the bad habit of falling behind, because I had the easy ability to catch back up. Here, assignments stack up fast across all of my classes. There is always something due at the end of the week, and if I ignore Canvas for even a few days, I will certainly fall behind. This term taught me that "I will do it later" usually means that it will not be done the way I want.
  • At the same time as all of this, I have started to build better habits. I'm almost forced to due to the immense lack of external help that I used to have. I use Canvas more consistently, I keep track of due dates with my personal calendar that is fed all the important information from all of my classes, and I am learning how to break bigger projects into smaller pieces so they feel less overwhelming. My time management has improved a lot already and I'm sure that a harder course load will necessitate that even more. I am still figuring out the balance between wschool, personal life, and taking care of myself, but I can see substantial progress compared to the beginning of the term.

Past Struggles and Motivation

  • In the past, my main academic struggle was procrastination. This was also very driven by my high school teachers' lack of urgency. I would put assignments off until the last minute because "who cares if it's a few days late?" My teachers will grade it the same anyways, and if There were more strict deadlines, then I would have to rush and rely on my natural ability to pull things together. That worked for a while, but it led to a lot of stress and I would rarely output my best work. I also never had the opportunity to seek help because teachers would not give me sympathy someone who turned it in on time might get.
  • My motivation for something has always been tied to how interested I am in it. If a topic felt meaningful or connected to my future, I could focus for hours. Coding always grabbed my attention so much better than anything else because it felt like I actually had a talent for it. If something I had to do for class felt pointless, I would often zone out or distract myself with my phone. This class helped me see that I cannot just wait to "feel motivated" before I start. I need systems and routines that push me to get my work done even when I'm tired or not in the mood.
  • Now my motivation is more connected to who I want to become. I need to be a reliable person who can show up to a high level, important job and handle the complex problems thrown my way. I can't be the person constantly scrambling, uncoordinated in my assignment and putting it off 'till the last moment. I keep thinking about my future, in a field like backend development, game development, or data science. These thoughts help me push through the less exciting parts of university, like readings or smaller busy-work assignments. Because I see them as part of a bigger picture.

SMART Goals (Draft Turned Real)

Six SMART goals that cover both academic and personal parts of my life. Each one has a clear timeline, a way to measure progress, and a reason that actually matters to me.

Academic / Career

By the end of this academic year, I want to earn a 4.0 GPA across my classes. To do this, I will check Canvas and my email multiple times a day, start major assignments when they are assigned, and put time into them days before they are due. I will study for exams in short chunks instead of cramming. I will review my grades weekly so I can adjust early instead of being surprised at the end of the term.

Timeline: September 2025 - June 2026, Full Academic Year

At least one will be a backend focused project, one will be a small singleplayer game, and one will involve data analysis or data visualization. I will set aside at least four hours per week for personal coding projects and update my GitHub activity at least twice a month.

Strategies for Achieving My Goals

Concrete strategies from this course and from my own experience that I plan to keep using as I move through college and into my career.

  • Using time blocking instead of "I will do it later." I plan to block out specific times in my week for classes, homework, coding projects, exercise, visting/calling my parents and rest. Instead of just hoping I find time, I will treat school work like appointments that I must show up for. This will help me avoid last minute stressful situations and procrastination. This also supports my goal of higher grades, staying connected to my family, and my exercise goals.
  • Checking Canvas and email every day. One major habit I learned in this course is to treat Canvas as the main hub of academic life. I will check Canvas and my PSU email throughout the day so I do not miss updates, and consistently on the weekends because most assignments are due on Sunday. This will help me stay ahead of due dates and announcements like class cancellations and important deadlines that might not be listed elsewhere. This will also help me with my procrastination issue because it won't allow me to miss important information.
  • Breaking large tasks into smaller steps. When I have a big assignment, project, or test, I will break it into smaller pieces such as reviewing with a friend, doing some practice problems or creating an outline for how I want to complete the task. I will then format these smaller tasks to span over a couple days, instead of doing it all at once. This makes big things feel less, and helps with my procrastination by making my work less overwhelming.
  • Using office hours, tutoring, and peer support. In the past I would avoid asking for help from my teachers unless I was in dire need. Moving forward, I plan to attend office hours at least once before any major exam or project in my Computer Science classes. I also plan to visit tutoring and or study labs if I ever feel stuck. I also have a friendly computer science study group where we work through problems together instead of trying to tackle complicated issues on our own.
  • Creating a better study environment. I'm very aware about the issue of having a screen with you while you're trying to complete work. Technology is so useful but can also be such a major distraction. To focus, I plan to study in quiet areas on campus or in my apartment with my notifications silenced. I will split my time into short focusd work sessions, similar to the Pomodoro method, with breaks built in. This should help me use my energy and time more efficiently instead of getting burnt out and then defaulting to procrastination.
  • Connecting coursework to my long term goals. It's hard for me to care about and assignment or class when it doesn't feel exciting. I will remind myself how core skills like writing, critical thinking and time management connect to my end goals. Seeing those links helps me stay motivated with the more boring aspects of education, when the work itself is not naturally intereseting.

What My Future Holds

How I plan to get the most out of my time in college and where I see myself in about five years.

  • Getting the most out of college. I want Portland State to be more than just a place where I crawl my way through classes for a degree. My plan is to actually use the resources available to me. Including but not limited to geting to know professors, going to office hours, using the library consistently for a quiet place to study and do my work, and joining at least one club relating to my major. I also want to stay active of course so the recreation center is the perfect place for me to do that. There are so many resources at my fingertips that I haven't taken enough advantage of yet.
  • Building real world experience. Beginning this coming summer of 2026 I plan to actively look for an internship or job relating to my major to help build real work experience and expand my portfolio. Since I am not currently working, I have some space to shape my schedule and begin applying soon. My goal is to apply to at least three opportunities each term and to treat each applicatoin as practice, even if I don't get the first few positions.
  • Five year vision. In about five years, I see myself as a more confident and experienced developer who has completed several internships or early career roles. I picture myself working as a backend software engineer or in a job that mixes programming with data analysis or data science, with a strong portfolio that shows what I can actually build. By that time I want to feel comfortable working on a team which is something I don't have a lot of experience in right now, reading and writing code used by others, and continuing to learn new languages and tools that will help my career trajectory.
  • Life outside of work. Of course life isn't only about work. And if I find a job that is more in tune with my goals but happens to pay less than alternatives it's likely I will take it. I imagine a life where I have a healthy routine, where exercise and time with family are normal parts of my week. I want to keep living in a city similar to Portland. I want to have the financial stability to travel, explore new hobbies, and keep learning new things, not just for a grade.
